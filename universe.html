Galaxies

Spiral Galaxy

Example:Milky Way

A galaxy with a flat disk and spiral arms full of stars, gas, and dust.

Elliptical Galaxy

A round or oval galaxy made mostly of old stars with little gas.

Irregular Galaxy

Example:Large Magellanic Cloud

A galaxy with no clear shape, often chaotic and rich in gas.

Lenticular Galaxy

A disk-shaped galaxy without spiral arms and with older stars.

Dwarf Galaxy

A small galaxy with fewer stars that often orbits larger galaxies.

Stars

Main Sequence Stars

Stars that are steadily burning hydrogen into helium in their core, like the Sun.

Red Dwarfs

Small, cool stars that burn fuel very slowly and can live for billions of years.

Blue Giants

Very hot and massive stars that shine brightly but have short lifespans.

Red Giants

Large, cooler stars that have expanded after using up most of their hydrogen fuel.

Supergiants

Extremely massive and luminous stars, such as Betelgeuse.

White Dwarfs

Small, dense remnants of stars that no longer produce energy through fusion.

Neutron Stars

Extremely dense stellar remnants made mostly of neutrons after a supernova explosion.

Pulsars

Rapidly spinning neutron stars that emit beams of radiation like a lighthouse.

Black Holes

Objects formed from collapsed massive stars with gravity so strong that nothing can escape.

The Solar System

Single-Star Systems

Example:The Sun with 8 planets (Mercury → Neptune).

A solar system with one star at the center, like our Solar System.

Binary Star Systems

Example:Alpha Centauri

Two stars orbiting each other. Planets may orbit one star or both.

Trinary or Multiple Star Systems

Example:HD 188753

Three or more stars bound together by gravity. Planets are rare due to complex gravity.

Circumbinary Systems

Example:Kepler-16

Planets orbit two stars at once. Sometimes called “Tatooine systems” after Star Wars.

Rogue Planets / Free-Floating Systems

Fact:They are extremely hard to detect.

Planets or planetary objects that do not orbit any star. They drift through the galaxy.

Protoplanetary Systems

Example:HL Tauri

Very young solar systems where planets are forming from gas and dust around a newborn star.

Compact Planetary Systems

Example:TRAPPIST-1

Systems where planets orbit very close to their star, often much closer than Mercury is to the Sun.

Exotic / Special Systems

Example:PSR B1257+12

Unusual systems like planets orbiting neutron stars or pulsars.

Moons

Regular Moons

Example:Ganymede, Titan

Moons that orbit their planet in a predictable, circular or elliptical path, usually close to the planet’s equatorial plane.

Irregular Moons

Example:Triton

Moons with eccentric orbits, often far from their planet, sometimes orbiting backward (retrograde). Usually captured asteroids.

Natural Satellites

Example:Phobos

General term for any moon orbiting a planet, dwarf planet, or asteroid.

Trojan Moons

Example:Some of Jupiter’s minor moons are trojans.

Moons that share the orbit of a larger moon or planet at stable points (Lagrange points).

Captured Moons

Example:Triton

Asteroids or objects captured by a planet’s gravity rather than forming with the planet.

Co-orbital Moons

Example:Saturn’s moons Janus and Epimetheus

Two moons sharing almost the same orbit around a planet.

Shepherd Moons

Example:Small moons that guide and shape planetary rings through their gravity.

Prometheus

Dwarf Planet Moons

Example:Charon

Moons orbiting dwarf planets in the outer solar system.

Binary Moons

Example:Pluto and Charon act almost like a binary system.

Two moons of similar size orbiting a common center around their planet.

Black Holes

Stellar Black Holes

Formed when a massive star (about 20 times the mass of the Sun) collapses at the end of its life.

Typically 5–100 solar masses.

Intermediate Black Holes

Black holes with a mass between stellar and supermassive, often hundreds to thousands of solar masses.

Harder to find; may form by the merging of smaller black holes or star clusters.

Supermassive Black Holes

Extremely massive black holes at the centers of galaxies, millions to billions of solar masses.

Influences galaxy formation and motion of stars around the center.

Primordial Black Holes (Hypothetical)

Tiny black holes that may have formed in the early universe due to high-density fluctuations.

Still theoretical; no confirmed observations.

Rotating (Kerr) Black Holes

Black holes that spin, twisting space-time around them.

Can drag matter and light along with the rotation (“frame dragging”).

Charged (Reissner–Nordström) Black Holes

Rare in nature because they quickly neutralize by attracting opposite charges.

Black holes that carry electric charge.

Kerr–Newman Black Holes

Theoretical black holes that are both rotating and charged.

Mostly studied mathematically; unlikely in reality.

Nebulae

Emission Nebula

Clouds of hot gas that glow brightly due to high-energy radiation from nearby stars.

Example: Orion Nebula

Reflection Nebula

Dust clouds that reflect the light of nearby stars rather than emitting their own.

Example: Pleiades Nebula

Dark Nebula

Dense clouds of gas and dust that block light from stars behind them, appearing as dark patches.

Example: Horsehead Nebula

Planetary Nebula

Shells of gas ejected by dying medium-sized stars (like our Sun) near the end of their life.

Example: Ring Nebula

Supernova Remnant

Gas and dust leftover after a massive star explodes in a supernova.

Example: Crab Nebula

H II Regions

Clouds of ionized hydrogen gas where new stars are forming.

Note: Often overlaps with emission nebulae.

Molecular Clouds

Cold, dense regions made mostly of molecules like H₂, the birthplaces of stars.

Example: Taurus Molecular Cloud
